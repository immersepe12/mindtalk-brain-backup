Talk therapy (psychotherapy) is an effective, first-line treatment for depression. NICE guidelines recommend psychological therapy — particularly cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) and interpersonal therapy — for mild-to-moderate depression, and evidence from Cochrane reviews and APA practice guidelines supports its use alongside medication for moderate-to-severe cases. Several different types of therapy have been studied and found to help, each working through a different mechanism. To find the right approach for you, [speak with a Mindtalk therapist](/contact) for an initial assessment.

## Does Talk Therapy Work for Depression?

The evidence is clear. CBT is among the most extensively studied psychological treatments for depression. A 2020 Cochrane systematic review covering 296 trials found CBT superior to control conditions for depressive symptoms. Interpersonal therapy (IPT) and short-term psychodynamic therapy also have strong evidence bases. The APA's clinical practice guidelines classify psychological therapy as a first-line treatment for depression, particularly when severity is mild-to-moderate.

For mild-to-moderate depression, NICE guidelines suggest that therapy alone — without medication — can produce outcomes comparable to antidepressants. A key advantage of completing a course of therapy is reduced relapse: people who engage fully with a structured course, such as 16 to 20 sessions of CBT, are less likely to experience a recurrence than those who stop after initial symptom relief.

## Types of Talk Therapy That Help Depression

Different types of therapy target depression through different mechanisms. A clinician chooses the approach most likely to help based on your history, the drivers of your low mood, and your own preferences.

### Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT)

CBT is the most extensively studied psychological therapy for depression and a first-line recommendation from NICE, the APA and the WHO. It works by identifying the negative automatic thoughts ("I am worthless," "things will never improve") and avoidance behaviours that maintain depression, and systematically challenging and replacing them. CBT is structured, goal-directed and typically delivered in 12 to 20 sessions. It is particularly effective for people whose depression involves a prominent pattern of self-critical or catastrophising thinking. Learn more about [CBT at Mindtalk](/treatments/cognitive-behavioural-therapy-cbt).

### Interpersonal Therapy (IPT)

IPT focuses on the relationship between depression and interpersonal stressors — grief, role transitions (such as job loss or parenthood), relationship conflicts, and social isolation. It helps you grieve losses, navigate life changes and improve the quality of key relationships, on the premise that improving interpersonal functioning relieves depressive symptoms. IPT is recommended by NICE for depression and has a strong evidence base comparable to CBT. It suits people whose depression is closely connected to a relationship difficulty or a major life change.

### Psychodynamic Therapy and Counselling

Psychodynamic therapy explores the underlying patterns, early experiences and unconscious processes that contribute to depression. Rather than focusing on present-day thoughts and behaviours, it helps you understand where low self-worth or persistent hopelessness came from. A 2017 Cochrane review found brief psychodynamic therapy more effective than control conditions for depression. It suits people who want to understand the roots of their mood rather than primarily focus on symptom management. Person-centred counselling, a related approach, offers non-directive, empathic support and can be valuable for mild depression or as a complement to more structured therapies. See Mindtalk's [talk therapy overview](/treatments/talk-therapy) for how these approaches are delivered.

## What to Expect and How to Start

A first session with a therapist — sometimes called an assessment or intake session — is a conversation about what you are experiencing, your history and what you hope to get from therapy. The therapist will ask about the onset, duration and severity of your low mood, as well as any previous treatment and relevant life circumstances. Nothing is expected of you except to be honest; the therapist's job in the first session is to listen and understand, not to provide instant solutions.

After the assessment, the therapist will recommend an approach and outline a course of treatment — typically a set number of sessions for structured therapies like CBT or IPT. Most sessions run 50 to 60 minutes. Weekly or fortnightly frequency is standard for an active phase of treatment; some people reduce frequency once significant improvement is established.

## Talk Therapy vs Medication for Depression

This is a clinical decision, not an either-or question. For mild-to-moderate depression, NICE supports talk therapy as a first-line standalone treatment with outcomes comparable to antidepressants. For moderate-to-severe depression, the evidence favours combined treatment: a 2014 meta-analysis in *PLOS Medicine* found combination therapy (CBT plus antidepressants) superior to either approach alone for chronic or recurrent depression.

Medication typically acts faster on the biological symptoms of depression — sleep, appetite, concentration — while therapy addresses the psychological patterns that maintain low mood and reduce relapse. Many people find that starting medication gives them enough lift to engage productively with therapy. A clinician and psychiatrist working together will advise based on your severity, history, personal preferences and circumstances — there is no formula that applies to everyone.
